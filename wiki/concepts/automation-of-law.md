# Automation of Law

The Automation of Law refers to the integration of artificial intelligence, algorithmic decision-making, and automated surveillance into legal systems, law enforcement, and governance structures. In the Metaviews corpus, this concept marks a critical shift from human judgment to algorithmic authority, raising profound questions about accountability, due process, and the erosion of democratic institutions. It matters to Metaviews because it represents a primary vector through which authoritarian control is being technologically encoded and normalized under the guise of efficiency and objectivity.

## In the Metaviews archive

Metaviews traces the evolution of the Automation of Law from early academic inquiries into robotics policy to a concrete political strategy for consolidating power. The archive begins with foundational discussions on the role of robots in society and the impact on regulation, notably at the #WeRobot2017 conference, which framed the initial legal questions surrounding automation. Over time, the focus shifts from theoretical policy to active implementation, particularly within the context of the Trump administration's second term, where automation is no longer just a tool but a foundational element of a new political order.

A central tension identified in the corpus is the trade-off between efficiency and accountability. Posts such as *The Automation of Intelligence and the Erosion of Accountability* and *The Perils of Automated Justice* argue that handing intelligence and policing functions to machines removes the human element required for nuanced judgment, amplifying biases while insulating decision-makers from liability. This is not merely a technological upgrade but a deliberate strategy to dismantle the mechanisms that keep authoritarianism in check, including the systematic targeting of legal advocates and the suppression of journalistic scrutiny.

The archive further documents how this automation is legitimized through legislative and financial means. Metaviews analyzes the integration of cryptocurrency into the U.S. financial system and the use of legislation like Ontario’s Bill C-5 and federal Bill C-2 as vehicles for "polite authoritarianism," where laws are used to make resistance impossible rather than relying on overt force. The concept culminates in the surveillance of interiority, where thought and private data are captured as input for infrastructural control, transforming the legal landscape into a system where the "guillotine" is no longer theatrical but embedded in the technology itself.

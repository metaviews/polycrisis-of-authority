# OpenAI and Anthropic

OpenAI and Anthropic are the vanguard artificial intelligence laboratories whose competitive race, safety debates, and corporate structures embody the centralization of computational power and epistemic influence. In the Metaviews archive, these entities are analyzed not merely as technology providers but as political actors shaping geopolitical stability, authority, and the ideological project of AI through strategic narratives and technocratic dominance.

## In the Metaviews archive

Metaviews covers OpenAI and Anthropic as central nodes in the concentration of AI power, framing their activities within the broader "AI arms race" that has become a critical juncture for national security and global stability. The archive highlights how these laboratories are inextricably linked to geopolitical competition, particularly between the U.S. and China, with U.S. institutions and the tech sector positioned as the primary instruments in this struggle. Coverage emphasizes the existential stakes of this race, noting that the pursuit of AI dominance drives massive resource extraction, including energy demands that threaten grid stability, while raising profound questions about who controls the underlying infrastructure of semiconductors and intellectual property.

A significant portion of the analysis focuses on the corporate narratives and epistemic strategies employed by these labs to consolidate authority. Metaviews critically deconstructs framing devices such as Anthropic's "Powerful AI" narrative and OpenAI's timelines for artificial general intelligence (AGI), interpreting them as strategic tools designed to legitimize the power of insiders steering AI development. The archive argues that these narratives serve to deflect scrutiny and manage public perception, effectively positioning the labs as the necessary arbiters of safety and progress while advancing a technocratic agenda. This scrutiny extends to the "ideological project" of AI, where Metaviews contends that these institutions drive a shift of authority away from individuals and communities toward centralized corporate and state structures, often leveraging "safety" and "ethics" debates to reinforce this centralization rather than mitigate it.

The coverage also connects OpenAI and Anthropic to broader themes of algorithmic authority, the erosion of accountability, and the rise of corporate technocracy. By linking these labs to figures like Peter Thiel and examining the militarization of AI, Metaviews portrays them as key drivers of a system where human judgment is increasingly automated and subordinated to opaque algorithms. The archive advocates for counter-movements such as participatory ethics and inclusive governance, arguing that the current trajectory of these laboratories threatens democratic authority and autonomy. Ultimately, Metaviews positions OpenAI and Anthropic as embodiments of the "economy of belief," where the currency of influence is manufactured through the control of information, the shaping of reality, and the monopolization of computational power.

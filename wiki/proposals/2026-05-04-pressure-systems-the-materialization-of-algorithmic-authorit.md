## Source excerpt
Algorithmic systems have moved past advisory and recommendation roles and now exercise material authority over resource allocation, access, and life outcomes. The "pressure systems" framing tracks how the accumulation of decision-power without deliberative legitimacy produces predictable brittleness when contested. Where these systems encounter publics that still expect institutions to answer for their choices, the gap between automated output and human accountability widens into structural friction.

## Why it matters for Polycrisis
This signal grounds the **legitimacy axis** of the Polycrisis of Authority simulation, specifically the failure pattern in which authority becomes operationalized before it becomes deliberatively grounded. It supports the advisor voice concerned with institutional realism — the contention that algorithmic governance does not displace political authority so much as absorb it into systems that cannot answer for themselves. Within the simulation, the entry seeds scenarios where routine infrastructure decisions (moderation, allocation, eligibility) cascade into delegitimization because the locus of authority has migrated into outputs that resist interrogation in the moment of exercise.

## Draft synthesis
The Polycrisis of Authority simulation tracks how overlapping authority crises — political, epistemic, infrastructural — interact rather than resolve sequentially. The materialization of algorithmic authority is a structural input to that simulation: as ranking, recommendation, eligibility, and moderation systems accumulate decision-power, they convert what was previously soft influence into hard constraint. [Algorithmic authority](../concepts/algorithmic-authority.md), in the concept sense, names the condition in which model outputs shape who is seen, heard, funded, or permitted; this signal asks what happens when that condition stops being reversible.

The post's pressure-systems framing fits the simulation's interest in feedback loops. Authority that cannot be questioned in the moment it is exercised tends to surface later as contested precedent — algorithmic decisions become the new ground rules precisely because they were never treated as decisions at all. This connects downstream to the [care-infrastructure signal](../signals/2026-05-05-algorithmic-authority-care-infrastructure.md), where the labor of living with automated authority falls on those least equipped to litigate it, and to the [epistemic-fracture signal](../signals/2026-05-08-algorithmic-authority-epistemic-fracture.md), where the authority to define what counts as knowledge migrates alongside the authority to act on it. Read together with the [future-of-authority](../concepts/future-of-authority.md) concept, these entries triangulate a posture in which legitimacy cannot be assumed to trail capability — and crisis becomes the default mode of governance rather than its exception.

## Source excerpt
Algorithmic authority does not merely distribute decisions faster; it produces epistemic fractures — splits between what a system knows, what it can justify, and what its subjects experience as legitimate. Repairing these fractures would require care infrastructures grounded in attentiveness, contextual judgment, and reciprocity. Such infrastructures cannot be built at the scale or speed at which algorithmic authority operates, and this mismatch is the crisis rather than a bug to be engineered away.

## Why it matters for Polycrisis
This signal sits on the simulation's epistemic-authority axis and grounds a failure pattern in which the legitimacy deficit generated by [algorithmic authority](../concepts/algorithmic-authority.md) cannot be closed by technical refinement alone. It supports an advisor voice that names care as the missing substrate of legitimate decision-making — a voice the simulation currently under-represents relative to its optimization and governance framings. Read against the [2026-05-05 care-infrastructure signal](../signals/2026-05-05-algorithmic-authority-care-infrastructure.md), it escalates the demand from diagnosis to structural impossibility.

## Draft synthesis
The post reframes epistemic fractures not as information problems but as relationship problems. When an algorithmic system decides, it implicitly claims knowledge about a situation it cannot inhabit, and the gap between claim and inhabitation is where legitimacy erodes. The author's move is to insist that only care — slow, situated, reciprocal — can fill that gap, and to note that care does not scale the way authority does.

This recasts the Polycrisis of Authority as a contest between a scalable form of authority and an unscalable form of repair, rather than as a competition among competing legitimate powers. The [theme page on AI and power dynamics](../themes/ai-and-power-dynamics.md) is the natural long-term absorber of this tension; for now the signal stands as a marker that the simulation must represent care-infrastructure collapse as a structural outcome, not a contingent side effect.

Together the two entries form a paired mapping of the impossibility condition — one diagnosing it in institutional terms, the other dramatizing it — and the curated wiki should eventually fold them into a single treatment of care-versus-scale as a primary fault line.

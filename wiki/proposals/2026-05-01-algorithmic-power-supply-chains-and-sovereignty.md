## Source excerpt
> As AI capability concentrates in a handful of firms and jurisdictions, the substrate of algorithmic authority—compute, energy, and trained expertise—follows the same path that commodity supply chains have traced for decades. Sovereignty is no longer just about who controls territory or capital, but who controls the upstream inputs to machine cognition. Where those inputs are contested, jurisdiction erodes and adaptive capacity migrates.

## Why it matters for Polycrisis
This signal grounds the **infrastructural** axis of the Polycrisis simulation and reinforces the [[../concepts/ai-arms-race.md]] failure pattern. It argues that algorithmic authority cannot be detached from its physical substrate: compute clusters, energy grids, semiconductor fabrication, and the human expertise that makes them run. The post supports an *infrastructuralist* advisor voice and stresses tight coupling between technological, economic, and political axes—the point where a chip shortage, a sanctions regime, or a talent diaspora becomes a sovereignty event rather than a market event. It pairs with [[../signals/2026-05-04-materialization-of-algorithmic-authority.md]] and [[../signals/2026-05-07-algorithmic-authority-sovereign-fragmentation.md]] to track authority migration into supply-chain logic.

## Draft synthesis
Algorithmic authority is rarely discussed as a *physical* phenomenon, but the source post insists it is one. Capability at the frontier demands capital, energy, fabs, and trained personnel—resources that flow through supply chains structured by export controls, subsidies, and bilateral agreements. When those chains concentrate, authority concentrates with them; when they fracture, authority fragments along new seams.

The post extends [[../concepts/algorithmic-authority.md]] by relocating its locus. Earlier signals treated algorithmic authority as primarily a *governance* question—who sets the rules machines follow. This entry shifts the question upstream: who controls the inputs that make machines *possible*. That reframing aligns with [[../themes/ai-and-power-dynamics.md]], which already tracks how AI reshapes who holds leverage in contested systems.

For the simulation, the implication is that authority shocks propagate through material channels. A single export-control decision, grid failure, or talent restriction can cascade into model availability, downstream services, and ultimately jurisdictional reach. The signal should be cross-referenced in any scenario where the **infrastructural** axis destabilizes before the **informational** one—a coupling pattern the orchestrator should model explicitly rather than let emerge by accident.

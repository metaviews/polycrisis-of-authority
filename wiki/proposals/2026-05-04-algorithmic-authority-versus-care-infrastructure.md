## Source excerpt

The post argues that algorithmic systems exercise authority in domains — content moderation, welfare eligibility, hiring, policing — where they replace or overrule human deliberation, while the surrounding care infrastructure (caseworkers, counselors, mediators) is being defunded. Authority migrates toward code as the legitimating substrate of recognition and repair thins, producing governance without ground: decisions without accountability, and intervention without care.

## Why it matters for Polycrisis

This signal grounds the **authority axis** of the Polycrisis of Authority simulation. It crystallizes the *delegation without accountability* failure pattern: authority is offloaded to algorithmic systems faster than citizens can build the institutions or recourse mechanisms that would make them governable. It most strongly supports the care-infrastructure advisor voice, which argues that AI-governance legitimacy crises cannot be resolved procedurally without rebuilding the human intermediaries who can contest, translate, and repair algorithmic outputs. It also pairs with the 29 April reading on algorithmic warfare, where care deficit and automated lethality compound one another.

## Draft synthesis

Algorithmic systems now exercise authority across consequential domains — welfare eligibility, moderation, hiring, lending, policing — but on a substrate of eroding care infrastructure. The expansion of [algorithmic authority](../concepts/algorithmic-authority.md) runs parallel to the defunding of caseworkers, counselors, mediators, and mutual-aid networks that once translated institutional decisions into intelligible ones. Authority migrates toward code as its legitimating ground thins.

Read alongside the 29 April [algorithmic warfare and care deficit](../signals/2026-04-29-algorithmic-warfare-care-deficit.md) signal, the pattern is consistent: automated decision systems scale faster than the human infrastructures that would contest them, and the gap widens during crises. The earlier-May [materialization of algorithmic authority](../signals/2026-05-04-materialization-of-algorithmic-authority.md) signal names the legal-formal drift; this one names its social condition — authority becomes unaccountable because the people and institutions that would hold it to account are themselves dissolving.

For the [future of authority](../concepts/future-of-authority.md), the implication is that legitimacy cannot be rebuilt through procedural reform alone. Algorithmic governance will require active investment in care infrastructure — staff, time, relationships — as a precondition for accountability rather than a downstream consequence of it. The signal should therefore load against simulation scenarios that assume AI governance can be stabilized by transparency, audit, or appeal alone, without rebuilding the human middle.

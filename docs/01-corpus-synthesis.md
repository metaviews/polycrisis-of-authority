# Corpus Synthesis — What the Metaviews Archive Gives Us

_Compiled from a read-through of the Metaviews wiki structure, the load-bearing concept and entity entries, the AI-and-power longitudinal themes, and the recent Pressure Systems signals from late April through May 2026._

This document is the bridge between the Metaviews corpus and the Polycrisis design. Its job is to make explicit what the corpus gives us — both in terms of usable material and in terms of editorial framing — so that subsequent design decisions (state model, interpretation grammar, crisis anatomy) can be drafted against concrete substance rather than abstractions.

The corpus is unusually well-suited to this project. The Metaviews archive is not "AI policy content assembled for a game." It is a sustained, decades-long intelligence practice that has been tracking the very questions the game asks — how authority moves, what legitimizes it, where it collapses, how language and infrastructure reshape it — and has developed its own analytic vocabulary for doing so. Polycrisis inherits that vocabulary. It does not need to invent one.

## What the corpus gives us, in four layers

### Layer 1 — The analytic framework: CIA (Cognitive, Institutional, Algorithmic Authority)

The most directly reusable contribution of the corpus is the **CIA Framework for Authority**, introduced in *Decoding Power: The CIA Framework for Authority* (2024-10-26) and elaborated across the late-2024 series on the Future of Authority. The framework posits that authority in the digital age operates across three domains:

- **Cognitive Authority** — the capacity to master signal amidst noise; to direct attention; to make meaning in environments of information abundance. This is the realm of the attention aristocracy, memetic warfare, narrative coherence, and the death of expertise.

- **Institutional Authority** — the formal apparatus of governance, law, regulation, and corporate technocracy. This is where legitimacy is canonically conferred, where transparency is supposed to live, and where the automation of law is now being actively contested.

- **Algorithmic Authority** — the invisible, automated governance embedded in platforms and AI systems. This is the realm of Frank Pasquale's black box, the automation of intelligence, and the "soft coup" of decisions embedded upstream in models and infrastructure rather than downstream in legislation.

The framework is *exactly* the lens the game's collapse mechanics need. The three collapse modes we identified — legitimacy, technical, narrative capture — map onto the three CIA domains. The game's state axes can be derived from them. The corpus has already done the conceptual work of separating these domains and tracing their interrelation.

**Design implication:** the game's state model is not designed from scratch. It is a *gameplay-ization* of the CIA framework. This is a meaningful inheritance because (a) it gives the game a defensible analytic spine, (b) it lets the shareable artifact cite the framework as part of its literacy claim, and (c) it lets the orchestrator extend or revise the framework in collaboration with the parent corpus rather than maintaining a parallel theory.

### Layer 2 — The state axes: what the corpus says authority actually is made of

Reading the corpus for what it tracks as *load-bearing* variables, five recurring axes emerge. These are not invented for the game; they are the dimensions the corpus itself treats as consequential for understanding authority under pressure.

**1. Legitimacy (public mood + elite posture)**
The corpus treats legitimacy as a fragile, multi-sourced construct: it comes from public mood, from elite alignment (whether the system of expertise and institutional actors accepts the regime's moves), and from narrative coherence (whether the regime's actions can be made to make sense). The corpus's "Looming Legitimacy Crisis" post and the Future-of-Authority series treat legitimacy not as a binary but as a *texture* — something that erodes unevenly across these three sources and collapses when their divergence becomes unmanageable.

**2. Fiscal slack (material capacity to absorb shocks)**
The corpus tracks this most explicitly in the AI Arms Race material: data center power consumption, semiconductor supply chains (TSMC chokepoints, helium disruptions via Hormuz), energy-grid stress, capital flows (UAE's oil-funded compute, the $2B Beijing-killed deal), and labor displacement ("May Day in the age of AI"). The corpus treats material capacity as *finite, contested, and geopolitical* — not a budget line but a web of dependencies.

**3. Elite alignment (whether the system of expertise and institutional actors holds)**
The corpus documents this in the "Purge at Fort Meade" coverage, in the AI lab leadership disputes (Musk v. Altman, the unresolved core question of the case), in the academic AI safety community's position relative to frontier labs, in the open-source community's relationship to corporate AI, and in civil society's relationship to state regulators. Elite alignment is *not* "everyone agrees"; it is whether the institutional actors who would normally mediate between regime and public continue to function as mediators.

**4. Ecological debt / material conditions (the slow-moving physical substrate)**
The corpus tracks this through the climate-polycrisis material, food sovereignty coverage, supply-chain analysis, the algorithmic-care-deficit framing, and the explicit attention to AI's physical footprint. The corpus treats ecological debt as the *axis where collapse is most delayed and most devastating* — exactly the texture the game's collapse modes need.

**5. Narrative coherence (capacity to make meaning, tell a story about what's happening)**
The corpus is dense with material on this: the "Authority of the Internet" (mapping how information moves), the "Authority is a Virus" thesis, the "Manufacturing Consent" / "Cognitive Miser" / "Memetic Warfare" cluster, the "Death of the Public" framing. Narrative coherence is the most fragile axis — it can collapse quickly, and its collapse signals the others cannot be far behind.

**The AI-specific axis the corpus surfaces:** the corpus also consistently tracks **capability frontier** — the actual state of what frontier models can do. This is visible in the corpus's coverage of Mythos, Daybreak, GPT releases, capability evaluations, jailbreaks, and "offensive cybersecurity" capability assessments. The corpus treats the capability frontier as a *moving target that no policy regime can keep up with*, which is precisely the texture the game needs for technical collapse.

**Design implication:** the state model has five core axes (legitimacy, fiscal slack, elite alignment, ecological debt, narrative coherence) plus one AI-specific axis (capability frontier). The corpus has done the conceptual work of separating these and tracking their interaction. The game's state-vector representation is a gameplay-ization of this analysis.

### Layer 3 — The crisis texture: how the corpus shows AI policy actually fails

The corpus is exceptionally good at showing how AI policy *fails in characteristic ways*. Reading across the recent signals, four failure patterns recur — and these are the failure patterns the game's crises should evoke:

**Failure pattern A — The upstream embedding.**
AI policy regimes get drafted against visible outputs (chatbot behavior, image generation, content moderation) while the consequential decisions are made upstream (training data selection, capability evaluation criteria, RLHF reward modeling, deployment architecture). This is the "Nobody Voted for This" insight: by the time a policy has a visible surface, the decisions are already made. *Corpus sources: Nobody Voted for This; How AI Centralizes Power and Undermines Autonomy; Authority in a World of Artificial Realities; Mythos AI coverage.*

**Failure pattern B — The compute and capability escape.**
Every regulatory regime on AI runs into a frontier capability that wasn't anticipated and a compute base that wasn't covered. The corpus documents this repeatedly: the Anthropic-SpaceX compute deal, the UAE's oil-funded compute expansion, US hyperscalers moving data through Iraqi oil pipelines, the TSMC chokepoint, the helium-supply disruption via Hormuz. *Corpus sources: AI Arms Race concept; Energy Crisis and the AI Arms Race; SpaceX Data Center Gambit; Regulatory Frameworks and the Fragmentation of Sovereignty.*

**Failure pattern C — The legitimacy-erosion cascade.**
Policy regimes that survive contact with capability nonetheless collapse because their claims stop making sense to the people they govern. The corpus documents this through the "Looming Legitimacy Crisis" framing, through coverage of how Musk v. Altman "proved that AI is led by the wrong people," through the Trump administration's pre-release AI testing mandate (and Data & Society's response to it), and through the algorithmic authority / epistemic fracture signals. *Corpus sources: Looming Legitimacy Crisis; Invisible Hand of Algorithmic Authority; Algorithmic Authority and Epistemic Fracture; Algorithmic Authority and Care Infrastructure; The Death of the Public.*

**Failure pattern D — The memetic and narrative capture.**
Policy regimes that survive both capability and legitimacy nonetheless collapse because their meaning is captured by an external narrative. The corpus tracks this through "Iran Is Winning the Vibe War," through the Murdoch media acquisition of New York Magazine and Vox Media Podcast Network, through coverage of "algorithmic knowledge curation," and through the attention-economy cluster. *Corpus sources: Iran Is Winning the Vibe War; One Nation; Manufacturing Consent in the digital age; Memetic Warfare; Authority is a Virus.*

**Design implication:** these four failure patterns are the *crisis anatomy templates* the game can use. Each generated crisis can be tagged with which failure pattern(s) it activates. The interpretation grammar can be drafted against these patterns: a player policy that "addresses" a failure pattern at one level (visible surface) but doesn't touch the others (upstream embedding, compute escape, legitimacy cascade, narrative capture) should resolve against the axis that *is* affected — and leave the others untouched. This is exactly the asymmetry the paragraph from the brief describes: "stabilizing what's visible while quietly weakening what sustains authority."

### Layer 4 — The actor cast: who the corpus says the game has to read

The corpus gives us a *cast of actors* the simulation has to make legible — because these are the actors whose positions the player has to read in order to govern. The corpus's entity entries on these actors (with key tensions documented) provide the substance for the simulation's "elite alignment" signal.

**Frontier labs:**
- **OpenAI** — Altman as geopolitical pivot; AGI timeline claims; "AI Bros" culture; militarization.
- **Anthropic** — "Powerful AI" narrative as legitimation strategy; Mythos model; compute dependence on SpaceX.
- **Palantir / Anduril** — Defense contractor AI; NHS data integration; kinetic AI; "Palantir Kinetics."
- **DeepSeek (China)** — Cheaper, smarter models; the AI Cold War; the Beijing-killed $2B deal.
- **xAI / Musk** — Litigation against OpenAI; orbital data centers; mixed political posture.

**States and regulators:**
- **White House / Trump administration** — Pre-release AI testing mandate; rollback of Biden-era rules; alignment with lab interests.
- **Cyberspace Administration of China** — Algorithmic crackdown; capital walls; Beijing-killed deals.
- **UK government (Starmer)** — Massive AI investment; bid for AI leadership; relationship with Altman.
- **European Commission** — Regulatory frameworks; AI Act; friction with US approach.
- **Canada (special case)** — Five Eyes pressure; soft annexation concerns; LINK as civic response.

**Civil society and counter-movement:**
- **EFF** — Surveillance resistance; internet shutdown circumvention; privacy posture.
- **Data & Society** — AI Civics; critique of pre-release testing mandate.
- **Bellingcat** — Open-source intelligence methodology; counter to platform opacity.
- **Open-source community** — Vibe coding; the "soft coup" of decentralized development; resistance to corporate enclosure.
- **Academic AI safety community** — Critiques of corporate framing; Berkman Klein's Mythos analysis.

**Media ecosystem:**
- **Murdoch media** — Acquisition of Vox Media Podcast Network + NY Mag; narrative consolidation.
- **X / Twitter** — Algorithmic amplification; Musk-era moderation shifts.
- **Foreign Policy, The Conversation, Wired, The Verge** — Reference press with different editorial postures the player can read.

**Adversarial and external actors:**
- **Iran (and "vibe war")** — Memetic warfare; disinformation in AI slop era.
- **Russia** — Internet shutdowns; drone-security justifications.
- **UAE** — Oil-funded compute; OPEC exit; sovereign AI investment.

**Design implication:** the simulation's "elite alignment" signal is not abstract — it draws from a documented cast of actors whose positions on AI policy are publicly recorded in the corpus. When a player's policy move shifts the simulation toward or away from an actor's position, *that's the move the player is making.* The corpus makes this legible; the game can make it playable.

## What the corpus does NOT give us — gaps that require design

For honesty, three gaps:

**Gap 1 — Player POV.** The corpus is written from the position of *intelligence observer*, not *governor*. The game inverts this. The corpus gives us the world to govern in; it does not give us the experience of being the one who has to govern. The interpretation grammar — how player moves *feel* under pressure, why some words land differently than others — has to be designed fresh. The corpus can be the LLM's grounding context for that grammar, but the grammar itself is the game's own claim.

**Gap 2 — Outcome closure.** The corpus tracks ongoing dynamics; it does not resolve them. The game requires resolution: the simulation has to end, the collapse has to surface, the artifact has to be produced. The corpus's open-endedness is honest about the world; the game's closure is necessary for the literacy device to work. This tension is real and worth designing deliberately rather than papering over.

**Gap 3 — Pedagogical voice.** The corpus is an intelligence practice. The game is a pedagogical artifact that wants to *teach* — to make legible the relationship between language and authority. The pedagogical voice is not the corpus's voice. The shareable artifact, in particular, has to balance the corpus's analytical rigor with the warmer, more invitational voice a shareable social artifact requires. This is also a design task the corpus does not do for us.

## What this means for the build order

With the corpus synthesis in hand, the next design moves can be drafted against concrete substance:

1. **State model, drafted against the five core + one AI-specific axes identified above.** Each axis needs a definition, a visible-signal layer (what the player sees), a hidden-threshold layer (what actually determines collapse), and an interaction pattern with the other axes (e.g., fiscal slack decay can drive ecological debt accumulation; legitimacy erosion cascades through narrative coherence).

2. **Interpretation grammar, drafted against 2–3 concrete crises and 2–3 concrete player moves per crisis.** The grammar should make explicit how a player's words resolve against the state vector, including how the LLM's reading is grounded in the corpus (specific wiki entries, signals, entity positions).

3. **Crisis anatomy, drafted using the four failure patterns as templates.** Each generated crisis should be taggable with the failure pattern(s) it activates, and the wiki entries that ground it.

4. **Shareable artifact, drafted against a hypothetical run.** The artifact's content shape should be testable against (1)–(3) before being designed in detail.
